All machine learning models, from the simplest logistic regression to the most sophisticated GPT, are rearview mirrors — they look at the road already traveled to predict the curve ahead. Nonlinear models are clearer mirrors, capable of discerning more complex terrain; large language models are mirrors that can see nearly every road ever recorded. But no matter how sharp the reflection, no mirror can show you what lies beyond an intersection that has never before appeared.

Our instinct to favor smoothness and reject noise keeps models reliable and robust on the vast majority of days — yet it also leaves us naturally blind to the 1% of true anomalies that matter most.

Acknowledging and accepting this limitation does not bring contentment — for our ultimate ambition is to predict the future. The march of history has never been a monotonic, linear progression. Accidental, sudden events can inflict immeasurable consequences upon the individuals living through them — even if, in the long river of history, those same events amount to no more than a single ripple.
